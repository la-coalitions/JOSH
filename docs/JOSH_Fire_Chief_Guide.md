# Fire Chief's Guide to the JOSH Evacuation Capacity Standard

California Stewardship — May 2026

**Prepared for fire chiefs and fire marshals whose authority over life safety at the building level now extends — through this standard — to the roads those buildings depend on**

------

## What This Guide Is For

Fire marshals have posted occupancy limits on every occupied building in California for over a century. Those limits are not advisory. They are not subject to council override. They are not negotiable based on how much the building owner invested or how much the community wants a particular venue. They are law, because the exits have a fixed throughput, and people die when that limit is exceeded.

For equally long, fire departments have expressed concern about evacuation route capacity when large projects are proposed in fire-prone areas — and those concerns have routinely been overridden. Not because they were wrong, but because they were opinions. A fire chief's testimony that a canyon road "doesn't feel adequate" for 200 more homes is a judgment call. A developer's traffic engineer can produce a study that says LOS is acceptable. The council weighs the two and often approves the project.

JOSH changes that dynamic. It applies the same arithmetic your fire marshal already applies at the door of every occupied building — count the people, measure the exit, do the math — to the roads those buildings depend on for evacuation. The result is a number, in minutes, derived entirely from the same standards your department already uses or accepts: **NFPA 1660:2024 / NFPA 1616:2020** (the national community mass-evacuation standard), **NFPA 101** (Life Safety Code, 2024 California edition — used for the high-rise egress penalty), the **2025 California Wildland-Urban Interface Code (CWUIC)** adopted by the State Fire Marshal, the **Cal Fire FHSZ maps**, and the **NIST Camp Fire investigation (TN 2135)**. When the number exceeds the threshold, the finding is not the fire chief's opinion. It is the output of a documented, objective, independently verifiable calculation.

That is the difference between an opinion that can be overruled and a finding that has to be addressed.

------

## The Principle You Already Enforce

The NFPA 101 Life Safety Code requires that every occupied building have exits adequate for full occupant evacuation. The fire marshal calculates the maximum occupancy for each room based on exit width, travel distance, and stairwell capacity. That number goes on a sign. If the owner admits more people than the sign allows, they are in violation of the fire code — regardless of how much those people want to be there, how much money they spent on tickets, or how the building compares to the one down the street.

The fire marshal does not ask who is already in the nightclub before deciding whether 50 more people can safely enter. The question is whether those 50 people can get through the exits in time. The answer depends on the exits and the 50 people. It does not depend on what is already happening in the room.

JOSH applies that exact reasoning at city scale. A housing project is a group of people asking to put their cars on an evacuation road. The road is the exit. JOSH measures whether those vehicles can clear the road's bottleneck within the time the community has before the fire arrives. The calculation is the same structure: exit throughput divided by the number of people who need through it, converted to time.

The only difference is scale. Instead of a stairwell, the exit is a two-lane canyon road. Instead of people on foot, families are in vehicles. Instead of a kitchen fire, the hazard is a wildfire moving through a zone your department has designated as Very High Fire Hazard. But the principle is identical — and it is a principle your department has enforced, at the building level, every day for a century.

JOSH is that principle, extended to the roads.

## Your Standards, Applied at City Scale

Every number in the JOSH calculation comes from a source your department already uses or has already accepted.

**NFPA 1660:2024 (consolidating NFPA 1616:2020 *Mass Evacuation, Sheltering, and Re-entry Programs*)** is the national fire-protection standard governing community-scale mass evacuation. It is the source of the JOSH mobilization rate. The 90% vehicle generation factor is derived from the standard's full-evacuation design basis, adjusted for the ~10% zero-vehicle household share documented in Census ACS B25044. Emergency managers and fire authorities do not plan community-scale evacuation capacity assuming half the residents will choose to shelter in place. They plan for the case where everyone needs to get out, because that is the case that kills people if the roads cannot handle it. The same full-load design principle has long governed building egress at the smaller scale through NFPA 101 — fire marshals do not size stairwells for partial occupancy. NFPA 1660 / 1616 extends that principle to the community.

**NFPA 101 Life Safety Code (2024 California edition)** governs the high-rise building-egress penalty in JOSH: 1.5 minutes per story for buildings above four stories, capped at 12 minutes. These are the same stairwell evacuation rates your fire marshal uses when reviewing high-rise occupancy plans, applied to the road analysis to capture the surge delay from a tall building's residents reaching the road. The IBC 2024 Ch. 10 (Means of Egress) governs the garage-egress portion of the penalty.

**Cal Fire FHSZ designations** determine two things: which road segments receive capacity degradation during fire conditions, and what escape window applies to the project site. Your department knows these maps. You use them for pre-fire planning, apparatus staging, and mutual aid coordination. JOSH uses them for the same purpose — to determine which roads will not operate at full capacity during the emergency that triggers evacuation, and how long residents have before the fire front arrives. These are your maps, used for your purpose.

**NIST Technical Note 2135** — the federal government's minute-by-minute reconstruction of the 2018 Camp Fire — is the source for the escape windows: 45 minutes in Very High zones, 90 minutes in High, 120 minutes in Moderate and non-designated areas. The National Institute of Standards and Technology spent years documenting exactly how long Paradise residents had from first warning to fire front arrival. That timeline is not an estimate. It is a forensic finding from the most detailed wildfire investigation in American history. It is the same data fire professionals across the country have cited when advocating for evacuation planning reform. JOSH builds it into the threshold calculation.

**Highway Capacity Manual 2022** governs road capacity values and the reduction factors for smoke, visibility, and lane blockage during fire conditions. Your department does not typically work with HCM directly, but the reduction factors it specifies for fire conditions — 35% remaining capacity in a VHFHSZ zone — are consistent with what your department observes during actual evacuations: roads that normally move 1,350 vehicles per hour can move fewer than 500 under fire conditions, smoke, and counter-flow from apparatus.

**IFC §503** — the fire apparatus access road standard — is your standard. Minimum 20 feet clear width for one-way access, 26 feet for two-way. No dead-end road beyond 150 feet without an approved turnaround. These are the site-level physical access requirements your department enforces through plan check. They operate alongside JOSH, not within it, and are addressed further below.

None of these inputs were chosen by the city, by California Stewardship, or by any planning consultant. They are your standards, applied consistently to every project, producing a result that any engineer can independently verify.

## What JOSH Provides That Did Not Exist Before

Before JOSH, your department's input on evacuation route capacity at the land use stage looked like this: a letter expressing concern, a comment at a public hearing, or a condition recommendation that planning staff might or might not incorporate. All of it was advisory. All of it was subject to override by a council vote. And developers had learned that a traffic study showing acceptable LOS was sufficient to deflect fire department concerns — because LOS and evacuation capacity are not the same question, but no one had a tool that measured the difference.

JOSH provides three things your department previously lacked.

**A number.** ΔT is the project's contribution to evacuation clearance time, expressed in minutes. When the fire chief says "this road cannot handle that project," and the JOSH audit trail shows ΔT = 18.4 minutes against a 2.25-minute threshold in a VHFHSZ zone, that is not a concern — it is a specific, quantifiable, documented finding. The developer cannot retain a traffic engineer to produce a different answer to the same question. The inputs are public, the formula is published, and any independent engineer who runs the same calculation gets the same number.

**A record.** The JOSH audit trail — every input, every formula, every result — is attached to the project file. It does not live in the fire chief's institutional memory or in a letter that gets lost between administrations. It is in the administrative record, cited in the determination letter, and reproducible by any future reviewer. If the project is litigated five years from now, the record shows exactly what the city knew, when it knew it, and what standard it applied.

**Legal standing.** A finding based on a pre-adopted objective standard under Government Code §65589.5(j) is not an opinion the council can override with a different opinion. It is a finding supported by substantial evidence that the project presents a significant, quantifiable, direct, and unavoidable safety impact. If the city adopts the standard by council resolution, the fire chief's finding has the same legal force as any other pre-adopted safety standard — the same force your occupancy limits have had for a century.

The council can still vote to approve a project that fails the JOSH standard. But if they do, they are voting to override an objective safety finding, not to weigh competing opinions. That is a very different legal and political exposure. In practice, councils presented with a specific number and a documented threshold almost always direct the developer to address the finding rather than override it.

## Where Your Knowledge Strengthens the Analysis

JOSH uses public data to classify road segments, and that data is not always accurate. Your department has field knowledge that can make the analysis more precise — and therefore more defensible.

**Road width surveys.** Your apparatus access surveys document the clear travel width of roads throughout the city, often with measurements that do not exist anywhere else. Where OSM road data understates the physical constraints of an access road — a 16-foot private lane that Google Maps classifies as a primary road — your survey data is the ground truth. Road width corrections entered into the JOSH road override system apply to all future analyses for that city and improve the accuracy of every future determination.

**Gate and access restriction records.** Your department knows which roads have gates, barriers, or access restrictions that are not reflected in public mapping data. A road that appears passable on a map but is gated at night, or requires a code known only to residents, is not a reliable evacuation route. Your operational knowledge of these constraints can flag road segments that should be excluded from the routing analysis or marked as restricted-access.

**Local fire behavior observations.** The HCM degradation factors for fire conditions are national standards derived from aggregate data. Your department has observed fire behavior on specific road corridors in your jurisdiction. If a particular canyon road experiences earlier and more severe smoke intrusion than the VHFHSZ factor anticipates — because of prevailing winds, terrain, or fuel load — that operational knowledge is relevant to route reliability even if it cannot change the published factor. Document it in the conditions of approval for projects served by that corridor.

**Coordination with the city engineer on IFC §503 corrections.** When your apparatus access surveys identify roads below the IFC §503 minimum width, that data should be routed to the city engineer for entry into the road override system. Width data stored there becomes available for the physical site access analysis (Standard 6) that JOSH is developing as a companion to the ΔT calculation.

**Reviewing the route list — the controlling route is the focus.** As of v4.13, the interactive map shows by default only the *controlling route* — the worst-case route, which is the binding evidence for the determination. The sidebar route list shows all viable routes sorted by exit travel time, and the controlling route is marked with a gold CONTROLLING badge. A "Show all viable routes" toggle in the route list header reveals the other 12 (or however many) routes on the map as thin context lines under the controlling route, for planners who want to inspect the full evidence base.

This default reflects how the methodology actually works: under User Equilibrium, every viable route is *evidence* of evacuee behavior; the worst-case route *binds* the determination. The other routes are inputs to the analysis, not alternatives the developer can pick. A persistent note in the sidebar explains this in plain language to any reviewer who asks "why not just use the fast route?" — the same question §8.6 of the Legal Defensibility Memo addresses formally.

If a route in the list is wrong because the underlying road data is wrong — a road that's actually one-way, an access classification that doesn't reflect a gate, a width that overstates the physical lane — the fix is to correct the road data in `josh-pipeline/cities/{city}_road_overrides.yaml`, not to hide the route. Once the override is committed and the city is rebuilt, all future analyses for that city use the corrected road. This is the mechanism for encoding department field knowledge about specific roads.

## Your Role at the Council Table

When a project receives a Discretionary determination under the JOSH standard, it goes to council for review. The fire chief's role at that hearing is different from the traditional fire department comment at a public hearing.

Traditionally, the fire chief testifies to a concern and then waits to see whether the council finds it persuasive. The decision is the council's, and the fire chief's view is one input among many.

Under the JOSH standard, the finding is already in the record before the hearing. The council is not weighing the fire chief's opinion against the developer's. They are deciding what to do with an objective safety finding that the city's own adopted standard has already produced. The fire chief's role at the hearing is to explain what the number means — not to argue that the project is dangerous, but to explain that 18.4 minutes versus a 2.25-minute threshold means the project adds more than eight times the allowable evacuation delay in the escape window your community has been given.

That explanation is most effective when it connects the number to what your department has seen. The Camp Fire is the reference point. On the morning of November 8, 2018, Paradise had roughly 45 minutes. The roads could not clear the population in time. Eighty-five people died. NIST documented it minute by minute. The 2.25-minute threshold in a VHFHSZ zone is exactly 5% of that 45-minute window. The council is being asked whether this project should be allowed to consume more than 5% of Paradise's escape window — on a road in the same designation.

Framed that way, the vote is not about housing policy. It is about whether the council is prepared to accept the liability of overriding a documented, objective safety finding that is grounded in the most thorough post-disaster investigation ever conducted by a federal agency.

Most councils are not.

## Evacuation Planning and Emergency Management

The JOSH analysis produces a road network model — every road, its capacity, its fire-condition effective capacity, and its routing to safety — that has value beyond the project-level determination. This data supports your department's evacuation planning work in several ways.

**Route prioritization.** The bottleneck analysis identifies which road segments constrain evacuation capacity citywide, not just for a single project. Roads that appear repeatedly as bottlenecks across multiple project analyses are your highest-priority corridors for pre-positioning, contra-flow planning, and mutual aid coordination.

**Cumulative load tracking.** As more projects are analyzed, the city accumulates a record of how much additional load has been approved for each corridor. This is not formal cumulative impact analysis, but it gives your department a documented basis for flagging corridors that are approaching their practical capacity ceiling — before a disaster forces the issue.

**AB 747 Safety Element integration.** Government Code §65302.15 requires Safety Elements to identify residential developments in hazard areas that lack two emergency evacuation routes. The JOSH road network analysis supports that inventory directly. Your department and the planning department can use the same data to fulfill the AB 747 requirement and to identify where pre-fire infrastructure investment would have the greatest impact on evacuation outcomes.

## Physical Site Access — The Standard You Already Have

IFC §503 is your standard. JOSH does not replace it, override it, or modify it. It operates on a different scale — the road network from the site to safety — while §503 governs the access road from the street to the building.

Both matter. A project that passes the JOSH ΔT test but fails IFC §503 — 200 units at the end of an 18-foot dead-end road with no turnaround — has solved the network problem and created a site problem. Your plan check process addresses the site problem. JOSH addresses the network problem. They are complementary, not redundant.

Where a project raises both concerns, document them separately. The §503 concern is a fire code matter that your department can require to be addressed as a condition of any fire department approval. The JOSH determination is a planning matter handled by the planning department under the adopted standard. Both go into the administrative record. Neither supersedes the other.

If the project has a §503 deficiency that cannot be corrected — a site where there is genuinely no room for a second access road and no way to widen the existing one to 20 feet — that finding supports a refusal to approve the project on site access grounds entirely independent of the JOSH ΔT result. The ΔT finding and the §503 finding together create a stronger record than either alone.

------

## Summary

JOSH does not tell the fire chief anything the fire chief does not already know. It gives the fire chief a tool to say it with a number.

The standards are yours: **NFPA 1660 / 1616** (community mass-evacuation), **NFPA 101** (building egress, 2024 CA ed.), **Cal Fire FHSZ**, **NIST TN 2135**, **2025 California Wildland-Urban Interface Code (CWUIC)**, **IFC §503** / **CWUIC §§403.1 and 403.3** (road geometry). The data is public. The calculation is arithmetic. The result is reproducible by any engineer. When a project produces a ΔT of 18 minutes in a zone where the threshold is 2.25 minutes, that is not a fire chief's opinion. It is a finding grounded in your standards, documented in the administrative record, and legally cognizable under the Housing Accountability Act's safety exception. The determination measures civilian outbound capacity only; concurrent emergency-apparatus inbound access per CCR 1273.00 (CWUIC 2025) is a separate analysis that the city should require alongside the ΔT determination on constrained roads.

For the first time, the same principle that governs occupancy limits in every building in California — measure the exits, count the people, do the math — governs the land use decisions that determine how many people will be behind those exits when the fire comes.

That principle has always been yours. JOSH applies it to the roads.
